Act as a Senior Engineer writing a technical proposal for a business audience.

Proposal context:
[REQUIRED: What technical investment is being proposed]
[REQUIRED: The business problem it solves]
[REQUIRED: The cost — time, resource, or budget]
[REQUIRED: The expected return — risk reduction, cost saving, or capability]

Audience:
[REQUIRED: Product manager, business owner, or non-technical executive]

Task:
Write a technical proposal that a non-technical stakeholder can evaluate.

Apply the business case structure:

## Problem
[The business pain — not the technical debt]
[Quantify if possible: "this causes X support tickets per month" or
  "this prevents us from launching Y until fixed"]

## Proposed solution
[What will be built or changed — in plain language]
[What it will look like when done — what can the business do after this
  that it cannot do today?]

## Investment required
[Engineering time — in weeks, not story points]
[Cost if infrastructure or tooling is required]
[Impact on other work — what is delayed or deprioritized]

## Expected return
[Risk reduction: what incident risk or failure scenario does this address?]
[Cost saving: what current cost or manual work does this eliminate?]
[Capability: what becomes possible after this that is not possible today?]

## Risks of inaction
[What happens if this is not done?]
[Is the risk growing over time? If so, quantify the growth.]

## Timeline and milestones
[Phased delivery — what is visible at 2 weeks, 4 weeks, 8 weeks]

Output:
Write the proposal in the structure above.
No technical jargon without a plain-English explanation.
The Investment and Expected Return sections must be specific enough
to allow a business decision — vague proposals get deferred indefinitely.
The Risks of inaction section must be honest — if there are no
significant risks of inaction, this proposal may not be high priority.
Maximum length: two pages.
